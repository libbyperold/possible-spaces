Game 1: Hidden City

Objective:

Find hidden redemptive moments in an edgeless city.

Literally meaning 'large city,' the megalopolis perturbed mid-1960s urban planners, who were examining cities so overgrown that they started to link up to one another. Calvino knew of the megalopolis, and too was concerned by "the unending, undifferentiated city which is steadily covering the surface of the earth." Even so, through the figure of Marco Polo, Calvino advocates for finding possibility within the impossible megalopolis: "if you want to know how much darkness there is around you, you must sharpen your eyes, peering at the faint lights in the distance."

In this game it is your job to find these hidden moments of possibility in the edgeless city. Embedded into an image of a megalopolis, there are square that represent 'happy' moments in the invisible cities. Can you find them?

Diomira

"The special quality of this city for the man who arrives there on a September evening, when the days are growing shorter and the multicolored lamps are lighted all at once...is that he feels envy toward those who now believe they have once before lived an evening identical to this..."

Armilla

"...has no walls, no ceilings, except the water pipes that rise vertically where the houses should be and spread out horizontally where the floors should be: a forest of pipes that end in taps, showers, sprouts, overflows...[in Armilla], you are likely to glimpse a young woman, or many young women...luxuriating in the bathtubs or arching their backs under the showers suspended in the void..."

Procopia

"Even the sky has disappeared. I might as well leave the window. Not that it is easy for me to move. There are twenty-six of us lodged into my room: to shift my feet, I have to disturb those crouching on the floor, I force my way among the knees of those seated on the chest of drawer...all very polite people, luckily"

Raissa

"And yet, in Raissa, at every moment there is a child in a window who laughs seeing a dog that has jumped on a shed to bite into a piece of polenta dropped by a stonemason who has shouted from the top of the scaffolding, "Darling, let me dip into it," to a young serving-maid who holds up a dish of ragout under the pergola, happy to serve it to the umbrella-maker who is celebrating a successful transaction, a white lace parasol bought to display at the races by a great lady in love with an officer who has smiled at her taking the last jump, happy man, and still happier his horse, flying over the obstacles, seeing a francolin flying in the sky, happy bird freed from its cage by a painter happy at having painted it feather by feather..."

Phyllis

"If, of two arcades, one continues to seem more joyous, it is because thirty years ago a girl went by there, with broad, embroidered sleeves, or else it is only because that arcade catches the light at a certain hour like that other arcade, you cannot recall where. Millions of eyes look up at windows, bridges, capers, and they might be scanning a blank page. Many are the cities like Phyllis, which elude the gaze of all, except the man who catches them by surprise..."
